Ragball

Physics-Based Sports Puzzle Game

Engine: Unity Role: Game Designer / Programmer Duration: 4 Weeks Platform: PC

Project Overview

Ragball is a unique physics-based sports puzzle game that combines ragdoll mechanics with soccer-style gameplay. Players control a floppy ragdoll character attempting to score goals while navigating challenging obstacle courses. The game emphasizes player skill, timing, and understanding of physics-based movement to create a fun and challenging experience.

The core gameplay loop revolves around launching your ragdoll character toward the ball, making contact, and directing it toward the goal while managing momentum and avoiding obstacles. Each level introduces new challenges that test different aspects of physics-based control.

Level Design

Level 01

Tutorial Arena

A simple open arena introducing players to basic ragdoll controls and ball physics. Players learn how to launch their character, make contact with the ball, and aim for the goal. Minimal obstacles allow players to focus on mastering the core mechanics without frustration. The level features clear visual indicators for controls and objectives.

Goals & Features

Project Goals

  • Create an accessible yet challenging physics-based gameplay experience
  • Implement robust ragdoll physics that feel responsive and fun
  • Design levels that gradually introduce complexity
  • Balance frustration with reward to maintain player engagement

Key Features

  • Physics-based ragdoll character controller with launch mechanics
  • Dynamic ball physics system with realistic collision responses
  • Obstacle variety including moving platforms, barriers, and hazards
  • Score tracking and star rating system based on attempts
  • Responsive camera system following both player and ball

Project Metrics

1 Core Level
5+ Mechanics

Solo Project

Challenges & Solutions

Ragdoll Control Unpredictability

Problem: Initial ragdoll physics were too chaotic, making it nearly impossible for players to consistently hit the ball or control their character's trajectory. Playtesters expressed frustration with the random outcomes.

Solution: Implemented a semi-predictable launch system with visual trajectory indicators. Added slight dampening to reduce extreme ragdoll flopping while maintaining the comedic physics feel. Created a "launch preview" that shows approximate landing zones, giving players more control without removing the physics-based challenge.

Camera System Challenges

Problem: The camera struggled to track both the player character and ball simultaneously, often leaving one or both off-screen during critical moments. Players lost track of objectives.

Solution: Developed a dynamic dual-target camera system that smoothly transitions between following the player and the ball based on game state. When the ball is moving toward the goal, camera prioritizes ball tracking. During player control, focus shifts to the character. Added zoom-out functionality when both targets are far apart.

Scope Management

Problem: Late in development, attempted to add new features and multiple levels, causing time crunch and incomplete implementation. Some systems were rushed and not fully polished.

Solution: Focused on perfecting one complete level rather than multiple unpolished ones. Prioritized core mechanics and level polish over feature quantity. This taught valuable lessons about proper planning and realistic scope setting for future projects.

Game Areas

Main Arena

The primary play area features a soccer-style field with a central ball spawn point and goal zone. The arena includes various obstacles and ramps that create interesting ball trajectories. Designed with clear sight lines to ensure players always understand the goal location and ball position.

Obstacle Course

Surrounding the central arena, obstacle elements include moving platforms, bounce pads, and barriers that add complexity to ball navigation. These elements are positioned to create multiple solution paths, encouraging player experimentation with different ragdoll launch strategies.

Final Thoughts & Learnings

Ragball was an excellent learning experience in physics-based game design and the importance of scope management. The project reinforced that player control and predictability are crucial even in physics-driven games. While the ragdoll mechanics are inherently chaotic, players still need to feel they have agency over outcomes.

The biggest lesson learned was about planning and time management. Adding features late in development created unnecessary stress and prevented proper polish. Going forward, I've learned to establish clear scope boundaries early and stick to them, ensuring whatever I build is fully realized rather than attempting too much. The feedback from instructors about planning ahead has shaped my approach to all subsequent projects.

Challenges & Solutions

Movement Speed vs Control Balance

Problem: Initial dash mechanics were either too fast (causing disorientation) or too slow (feeling sluggish and unresponsive). Finding the sweet spot between exciting speed and maintainable control proved challenging during early playtests.

Solution: Implemented a momentum-based dash system with acceleration curves rather than instant max speed. Added visual motion blur and camera shake tuned to enhance speed sensation without actual disorientation. Extensive playtesting with different player skill levels helped identify the optimal speed values that felt fast yet controllable.

Weapon Balance Across Playstyles

Problem: Different weapon types dominated at various skill levels. Shotguns were overpowered for new players, while sniper rifles dominated high-level play, creating balance issues that varied by player skill.

Solution: Redesigned weapons around risk-reward tradeoffs rather than pure damage output. Shotguns require close-range risk, snipers have movement penalties while scoped. Implemented weapon switching cooldowns to prevent instant-swap tactics. Created "weapon triangle" balance where each type has clear counters, encouraging tactical loadout choices and mid-combat adaptation.

Steam Release Optimization

Problem: Preparing for commercial Steam release required optimization and polish beyond typical student project scope. Performance issues on lower-end hardware and missing quality-of-life features threatened launch quality.

Solution: Conducted extensive optimization pass focusing on LOD systems, particle effects, and draw call reduction. Implemented comprehensive graphics settings menu with presets. Added Steam achievements, cloud saves, and controller support. Multiple rounds of QA testing across different hardware configurations ensured smooth experience for target audience.

Combat Zones & Design Philosophy

High-Ground Positions

Elevated positions in each arena provide tactical advantages but expose players to flanking routes. These areas reward skilled movement and positioning while preventing them from becoming dominant camping spots through limited cover and multiple access points.

Central Control Points

Each arena features a central hotspot designed to become the focal point of combat. These areas provide tactical benefits (weapon spawns, score zones) that draw players together, ensuring consistent action and reducing the chance of boring matches.

Flanking Routes

Side passages and alternate paths allow players to outmaneuver opponents and break defensive positions. These routes are designed to be slightly riskier but reward strategic thinking and map knowledge, adding depth to combat beyond pure mechanical skill.

Hazard Zones

Environmental hazards like energy fields and pitfalls add tactical considerations to movement. Players can use these to their advantage by baiting opponents into danger or creating escape routes, adding another layer to combat decision-making.

Challenges & Solutions

Difficulty Balancing Across Skill Levels

Problem: Early playtesting revealed significant difficulty gaps. New players struggled with early levels while experienced players found later sections too easy. Balancing combat encounters to accommodate different skill levels proved challenging.

Solution: Implemented adaptive difficulty system that scales enemy stats based on player performance. Added optional tutorial sections that can be skipped by experienced players. Created multiple difficulty presets with clear descriptions of what each offers, allowing players to choose their preferred experience.

Team Coordination and Asset Pipeline

Problem: As a team project, coordinating between designers, programmers, and artists created workflow bottlenecks. Version control conflicts and inconsistent asset naming caused integration issues.

Solution: Established clear naming conventions and folder structures from project start. Implemented daily stand-ups and weekly milestone reviews. Created shared documentation for all systems and assets. This improved workflow significantly and taught valuable lessons about production pipelines.

Combat Feel and Player Feedback

Problem: Initial combat felt unresponsive and lacked impact. Players reported difficulty understanding when attacks landed or when they took damage. The combat system worked functionally but felt unsatisfying.

Solution: Enhanced visual and audio feedback with hit sparks, sound effects, and screen shake. Added brief hit-pause on successful attacks to emphasize impact. Implemented damage numbers and health bar updates with smooth animations. These juice elements transformed combat from functional to satisfying.

Game Areas

Starting Village

A safe tutorial area introducing players to basic mechanics. NPCs provide guidance and simple quests. The peaceful atmosphere contrasts with later dangerous areas, establishing the stakes for the journey ahead.

Boss Arenas

Dedicated combat spaces designed around specific boss mechanics. Each arena features environmental elements that can be used tactically during fights. The circular design ensures bosses remain visible while providing cover points.

Secret Chambers

Hidden areas rewarding thorough exploration. These chambers contain powerful equipment and lore items. Environmental puzzles guard entrances, ensuring only observant players discover them.

Transition Zones

Challenges & Solutions

Balancing Fear and Frustration

Problem: Initial playtests revealed players either found the game too scary to continue or not scary enough to be engaging. The line between effective horror and frustrating difficulty proved difficult to find. Some players quit due to stress while others felt bored.

Solution: Implemented pacing that alternates intense horror sequences with calmer exploration periods. Added safe rooms where players can breathe and manage resources. Created optional difficulty settings that adjust enemy aggression and resource scarcity. This allowed players to tailor the experience to their horror tolerance while maintaining core tension.

Environmental Storytelling Clarity

Problem: Early versions relied too heavily on environmental storytelling, leaving many players confused about objectives and narrative. The horror atmosphere made exploration difficult, causing players to miss crucial story elements and become lost.

Solution: Added subtle environmental cues guiding players without breaking immersion. Implemented an optional objective tracker that doesn't hold hands but prevents complete confusion. Created more intentional lighting directing attention to important areas. Balanced environmental storytelling with clearer narrative beats through audio logs and documents.

Performance and Atmosphere Balance

Problem: The atmospheric effects crucial to horror (complex lighting, particle effects, post-processing) caused significant performance issues. Frame rate drops broke immersion and made the game unplayable on lower-end systems.

Solution: Optimized lighting by baking static lights and using light probes effectively. Reduced particle effects where they weren't crucial to atmosphere. Implemented scalable graphics settings allowing players to prioritize performance or visuals. Learned valuable lessons about optimization without sacrificing artistic vision.

Horror Design Elements

Safe Rooms

Sanctuary spaces where players can save progress, manage inventory, and recover. These rooms provide necessary respite from constant tension while their rarity makes each discovery meaningful. The contrast between safety and danger heightens overall horror effectiveness.

Contaminated Zones

Areas where organic corruption has taken over, featuring disturbing visual design and unique hazards. These zones test player courage and resource management as protective equipment depletes. The grotesque environments reinforce body horror themes central to the game.

Dark Passages

Narrow corridors with minimal lighting where visibility is severely limited. These areas create claustrophobic tension and force players to use limited light sources strategically. Sound design becomes crucial for detecting threats in darkness.

Experimentation Chambers

Large rooms filled with horrific medical equipment and evidence of failed experiments. These spaces provide environmental storytelling while serving as arenas for more aggressive enemy encounters. Players must use the environment tactically to survive.

Final Thoughts & Learnings

Flesh Colour Horror was my first attempt at designing a horror experience and taught me invaluable lessons about pacing, atmosphere, and player psychology. Creating effective horror requires understanding what makes people uncomfortable and how to maintain that feeling without crossing into frustration or desensitization.

The project demonstrated the importance of playtesting with diverse audiences. Horror is subjective - what terrifies one player might bore another. Learning to design systems that accommodate different fear tolerances while maintaining core vision was challenging but crucial. The optional difficulty settings and safe room system emerged from this understanding.

Most significantly, this project taught me about environmental storytelling and using game mechanics to reinforce themes. The resource scarcity, sanity system, and organic corruption spreading through levels all served the body horror concept. Future projects benefited from these lessons about mechanical and thematic cohesion.

Mini Games Collection

Collection Overview

This collection represents various mini-game prototypes developed to explore different game mechanics, genres, and design philosophies. Each project served as a focused experiment in specific aspects of game design, from core mechanics to player psychology. These rapid prototypes allowed for quick iteration and learning.

The mini-games span multiple genres including puzzle, action, rhythm, and strategy. Each was designed with a single core mechanic in mind, polished to feel satisfying, then expanded with additional features. This approach taught valuable lessons about core gameplay loops and the importance of solid foundations.

Featured Mini Games

Game 01

Puzzle Platformer

A physics-based puzzle platformer exploring manipulation of gravity and momentum. Players rotate the world to navigate obstacles and reach goals. Focused on teaching the core mechanic through level design rather than tutorials. Featured 15 increasingly complex levels demonstrating mechanical depth.

Game 02

Rhythm Action

A rhythm game combining music timing with combat mechanics. Players defend against waves of enemies by attacking in sync with background music. Explored how audio feedback can guide player actions and create flow states. Implemented dynamic difficulty that adjusts to player performance.

Game 03

Tower Defense Variant

An experimental tower defense game where players can't directly place towers. Instead, environmental manipulation creates defensive positions. This constraint forced creative problem-solving and planning. Tested how removing player control in strategic ways can increase engagement.

Game 04

Stealth Prototype

A top-down stealth game focusing on line-of-sight mechanics and AI behavior. Players navigate through enemy patrols using timing and environment. Explored how clear communication of game state (enemy vision cones, sound radius) improves stealth gameplay satisfaction.

Game 05

Resource Management

A minimal strategy game about balancing competing resource needs. Players manage a settlement where every action has opportunity costs. Focused on meaningful choices and visible consequences. Demonstrated how constraints create engaging decision-making.

Game 06

Physics Sandbox

An experimental physics playground allowing players to create Rube Goldberg machines. No explicit goals - players set their own objectives. Explored emergent gameplay and how providing tools without prescribed solutions can engage creative players.

  • Explore different genres and design philosophies
  • Practice finishing and polishing small-scope projects
  • Build portfolio demonstrating versatility and range
  • Learn from both successful and failed experiments

Challenges & Solutions

Scope Creep in Rapid Prototypes

Problem: Initial mini-games suffered from feature creep as interesting ideas emerged during development. Projects intended to take one week expanded to three or four, defeating the purpose of rapid prototyping and preventing exploration of other concepts.

Solution: Implemented strict time limits and feature lists before starting each project. Created a "future ideas" document where interesting features could be noted for later projects without derailing current work. Learned to recognize when a prototype proved its concept and could be finished, even if more features were possible.

Maintaining Quality in Quick Development

Problem: Rapid development timeframes tempted cutting corners on code quality and architecture. Early prototypes became unmaintainable messes that couldn't be easily expanded or debugged when issues arose.

Solution: Developed personal code templates and best practices that could be quickly implemented in new projects. Created reusable component libraries for common functions (input handling, audio management, etc.). Learned that good architecture saves time in the long run even in short projects.

Learning from Failed Prototypes

Problem: Not every prototype succeeded - some core mechanics simply weren't fun or interesting despite seeming good on paper. Early tendency was to force these projects to completion or abandon them feeling like failures, missing learning opportunities.

Solution: Reframed "failed" prototypes as valuable learning experiences. Created post-mortems for unsuccessful projects analyzing what didn't work and why. This documented knowledge helped avoid similar pitfalls in future projects. Learned that quickly identifying and abandoning unsuccessful mechanics is a valuable skill, not a failure.

Key Learnings by Genre

Puzzle Design

Learned the importance of teaching mechanics through level design rather than explicit tutorials. Good puzzles have a clear "aha!" moment when the solution clicks. Difficulty should come from combining familiar elements in new ways, not from obscure or unintuitive solutions.

Action Games

Discovered the critical importance of responsive controls and clear visual feedback. Even tiny delays between input and action break the connection between player and character. "Game feel" matters more than complexity - simple actions that feel good beat complex actions that feel sluggish.

Strategy Games

Understood that meaningful choices require visible trade-offs. Players need to understand consequences of decisions before making them. The best strategy games don't have "correct" solutions but rather different approaches with distinct advantages and disadvantages.

Physics-Based Games

Learned that physics systems need constraints to feel good. Completely realistic physics can feel chaotic and uncontrollable. The key is finding the right balance between realism and predictability that allows players to master the systems.

Rhythm Games

Discovered that audio-visual synchronization is crucial for rhythm gameplay. Even small delays between sound and visual feedback break player flow. Clear visual and audio cues before required actions allow players to anticipate and enter flow states.

Sandbox/Emergent Gameplay

Understood that emergent gameplay requires robust, interacting systems. Players will find solutions designers never intended - this should be encouraged, not prevented. The designer's role shifts from creating puzzles to creating interesting tools and seeing what players build.

Final Thoughts & Learnings

This collection of mini-games represents my philosophy of learning through doing. Rather than spending excessive time in pre-production on untested ideas, rapid prototyping allowed me to quickly validate or invalidate design concepts. The failures taught as much as the successes - sometimes more.

The diverse genres explored expanded my design vocabulary and versatility. Working across puzzle, action, strategy, and rhythm games taught me to recognize patterns and principles that transcend genre. Core concepts like feedback loops, difficulty curves, and player agency apply universally, even if their implementation differs dramatically between genres.

Most importantly, these projects taught me about finishing things. It's easy to start new projects when existing ones become difficult. Learning to take prototypes from interesting concept to polished, complete (if small) experience was invaluable. The discipline of finishing projects, even when they're imperfect, has proven crucial in all my subsequent work.

The reusable code libraries and design patterns developed during this period continue to accelerate my work on new projects. Building a personal toolkit of proven solutions allows me to focus creative energy on novel problems rather than reinventing common systems. This collection represents the foundation upon which all my later projects were built.

Project Overview

Frenemies is a 2.5D local co-op puzzle game for three players. The game takes place inside the belly of a dragon who has eaten a chunk of the kingdom's castle along with our protagonists. Now our three brave heroes must find a way to defeat the giant beast.

Fortunately for them, upon being swallowed, our heroes found three magic hats, which grant the user a unique special ability when worn: a mage hat that allows them to freeze, an archer hat with a bow included and a barbarian hat with a strong hammer. Weirdly enough it seems like these abilities are supposed to be used on your friends! Freeze, Impale on walls or swing your friend like a golf ball! Embark in this "friendly" adventure with two others until you reach the dragon's heart, slay the dragon, and save the kingdom.

Challenges & Solutions

VFX Performance Optimization

Problem: Initial VFX implementations were causing significant performance drops, especially with multiple players and complex shader effects running simultaneously. Frame rates dropped below acceptable levels during intense gameplay moments.

Solution: Optimized shader code using HLSL to reduce computational overhead. Implemented object pooling for particle effects and created LOD (Level of Detail) systems for VFX that scaled based on distance and importance. This maintained visual quality while ensuring smooth 60fps gameplay even with all three players active.

Co-op Puzzle Balance

Problem: Designing 20 unique obstacles that required genuine teamwork while remaining solvable proved challenging. Early prototypes were either too easy (solved individually) or too difficult (frustrating for players).

Solution: Developed a systematic approach to obstacle design with clear roles for each character ability. Created playtesting protocols that identified when puzzles were too complex or too simple. Iterated on obstacle designs to ensure each required at least two players working together, creating natural moments of cooperation and communication.

Shader Learning Curve

Problem: Had limited experience with HLSL and shader programming at the start of the project. Needed to create custom shader effects for visual feedback but lacked the technical knowledge to implement them effectively.

Solution: Dedicated time to learning HLSL fundamentals and Unity's Shader Graph system. Started with simple effects and gradually built complexity. Created reusable shader functions that could be applied across different VFX, building a library of effects that accelerated development. The learning process became a valuable skill that enhanced the project's visual quality.

Technical Level Design Systems

Problem: Managing 20+ unique obstacles required robust systems for placement, testing, and iteration. Without proper tools, level design became time-consuming and error-prone.

Solution: Developed custom editor tools and systems for rapid obstacle prototyping and placement. Created modular obstacle components that could be easily configured and tested. This technical foundation allowed for quick iteration and enabled the team to focus on gameplay refinement rather than manual setup tasks.

Design Elements

Character Abilities System

Designed three distinct character abilities that create unique gameplay interactions: Mage (freeze ability), Archer (impale/wall interaction), and Barbarian (swing/hammer mechanics). Each ability was balanced to be useful in different scenarios while encouraging players to work together.

Obstacle Variety

Created 20+ unique obstacles that test different aspects of co-operative gameplay. Each obstacle requires specific character abilities and encourages communication between players. Obstacles range from simple platforming challenges to complex multi-step puzzles requiring all three players.

Visual Feedback Systems

Implemented comprehensive VFX systems using custom shaders to provide clear visual feedback for player actions. Effects include ability indicators, interaction highlights, puzzle state visualization, and environmental storytelling elements that guide players through the dragon's interior.

2.5D Art Direction

The game's 2.5D perspective creates a unique visual style that combines 2D character sprites with 3D environments. This design choice allows for depth perception while maintaining the charm of 2D art, creating an immersive experience inside the dragon's belly.

Final Thoughts & Learnings

Frenemies was an excellent opportunity to combine technical design with visual effects creation. My task for this project was to prototype 20 obstacles for the level design, and I was able to handle the majority of the level design's technical aspects. This project taught me the importance of creating robust systems early in development that support rapid iteration and testing.

I created all of the VFX for this project, including the custom shader graph functions. I picked up HLSL quickly and began working on shaders, which improved the project's visual feedback significantly. Learning shader programming opened up new possibilities for visual communication and player feedback that I hadn't explored before.

One of the biggest learnings from this project was understanding how VFX can enhance gameplay communication. Visual effects aren't just decorative—they serve crucial functions in guiding players, indicating game state, and providing feedback for actions. Balancing technical implementation with creative visual design became a key skill that I've applied to all subsequent projects.

The co-operative puzzle design process taught me valuable lessons about player psychology and communication. Designing obstacles that require genuine teamwork while remaining fun and solvable required extensive playtesting and iteration. This experience reinforced the importance of player feedback in the design process and how small adjustments can dramatically improve the player experience.
